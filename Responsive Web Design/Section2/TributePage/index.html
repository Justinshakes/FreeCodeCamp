Bell Hooks

Influential Black feminist writer, scholar, and cultural critic.

Bell Hooks speaking on campus at UW-Madison. Photo via the University of Wisconsin-Madison library.

A leading feminist activist and thinker, Bell Hooks (MA’76) began writing about race and gender issues at 19 years old when she wrote "Ain’t I a Woman." She was a provocateur and a passionate advocate for freedom of expression. She passed away at age 69 in December 2021.

Timeline of Bell Hooks:

  • 1952: Born Gloria Jean Watkins on September 25 in Hopkinsville, Kentucky.
  • 1973: Graduated from Stanford University with a bachelor's degree in English.
  • 1976: Earned a master's degree in English from the University of Wisconsin-Madison.
  • 1981: Published her first major work, "Ain't I a Woman? Black Women and Feminism," which critically examined the intersection of race, gender, and class in feminism.
  • 1984: Published "Feminist Theory: From Margin to Center," expanding her critique of feminist theory.
  • 1989: Released "Talking Back: Thinking Feminist, Thinking Black," a collection of essays on feminism and activism.
  • 1994: Published "Outlaw Culture: Resisting Representations," exploring pop culture and its impact on social norms.
  • 2000: Released "All About Love: New Visions," a book examining the nature of love and relationships.
  • 2010: Published "Teaching Critical Thinking: Practical Wisdom," focusing on the importance of education and critical thinking.
  • 2015: "Writing Beyond Race: Living Theory and Practice" was published, highlighting her evolving views on race and identity.
  • 2021: Bell Hooks passed away on December 15, leaving a lasting legacy in feminist and social justice scholarship.
